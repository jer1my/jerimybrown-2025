I keep hearing people say things like... UI is going away. Interfaces are becoming obsolete. The future is just you and a blinking cursor and the infinite wisdom of a large language model. We are moving beyond the need for buttons and menus and all that visual noise cluttering up our perfectly frictionless AI future.

And then on April 17, 2026 Anthropic launched Claude Design. Prototypes. Slides. Wireframes. Visual assets. All built around their AI with a thoughtful interface on top of it.

The pattern is not new. It just keeps repeating itself. And if you know where to look, the historical record makes the argument better than any opinion piece ever could.

The Green Screen Nobody Could Use

Let us go back to 1977. Three computers hit the market that year and changed everything: the Apple II, the Commodore PET, and the Tandy TRS-80. The home computer era had arrived. Technically.

The people buying them were hobbyists, engineers, early adopters, and the genuinely curious. To use one you needed to know your way around a command line. You needed to remember syntax. You needed to speak the machine's language because the machine was not going to meet you halfway. By 1982 there were an estimated 621,000 home computers in American households. In a country of 230 million people that is not a revolution. That is a niche.

Then in 1984 Apple launched the Macintosh. The first successful mass-market computer with a graphical user interface and a mouse. Icons. Menus. Windows. A visual layer that let anyone sit down and start doing something without memorizing a single command. By the turn of the 21st century text-mode operating systems were no longer a significant fraction of the personal computer market. US household computer ownership rose from roughly 8 to 10 percent in 1984 to around 50 percent by the late 1990s. The technology did not change. The interface did. And the market followed.

The Phone You Could Not Type On

Fast forward to the early 2000s. Cell phones existed. Texting existed. But have you tried to type on a T9 keypad? Three presses to get the letter C. Four presses to get the letter S. A physical act of frustration masquerading as communication. The technology was there. The interface was not.

In 2007 Apple launched the iPhone. A touchscreen that responded to your finger. A keyboard that appeared when you needed it and disappeared when you did not. An interface that felt like the phone was designed for a human being instead of a telecom engineer. When the iPhone launched only 6 percent of the US population had a smartphone. The smartphone then reached 40 percent market saturation in just two and a half years, pacing the television as the fastest adopted consumer technology in history.

The story was not limited to one country. Today there are 5.78 billion unique mobile users worldwide representing 70 percent of the global population. Markets like India, Indonesia, and Vietnam are seeing some of the fastest adoption rates on the planet, driven largely by affordable touchscreen devices that made smartphones accessible to people who had never owned a personal computer. The interface did not just unlock a market. It unlocked the world.

The Chat Box That Became Everything

November 2022. OpenAI launched ChatGPT as a text interface. A box. You type. It responds. It was remarkable and it was also immediately obvious that most people had no idea what to do with it. ChatGPT reached 1 million users in five days, which was unprecedented. But look at who those users were. Developers. Researchers. Writers. Technical early adopters who already knew how to construct a prompt and interpret a response.

Then the interfaces arrived. Memory. Projects. Image generation. Voice mode. Canvas. Claude added artifacts, tool integrations, and visual layers. Midjourney abandoned Discord slash commands for a full web UI. And adoption stopped being a technology story and started being a mainstream story. ChatGPT now has 900 million weekly active users. On April 17, 2026 Anthropic launched Claude Design. The chat box did not go away. It just stopped being the only thing in the room.

The pattern is identical to the PC. Identical to the smartphone. Every single time.

Transient UI Is Still UI

Here is where the argument gets more nuanced and more interesting. When people say UI is going away I think what they are actually observing is that interfaces are becoming more contextual. More situational. A heads up display that appears when you need it and disappears when you do not is still a UI. A voice response that generates a visual summary is still a UI. An AI that surfaces a button at exactly the right moment in a workflow instead of making you navigate a menu to find it is still a UI. It is just a smarter one.

Transient does not mean absent. Contextual does not mean gone. The interface is evolving the same way it always has, toward less friction, more relevance, and deeper integration with how people actually think and work. That is not the death of UI. That is UI doing its job.

We Are Visual Animals

This is the argument nobody seems to be making and it is the one I find most compelling. Humans did not develop eyes as a secondary sense. Vision is our primary survival mechanism. We evolved to navigate the world visually before we process it any other way. Our brains devote more processing power to vision than to all other senses combined.

The idea that a species that evolved to survive through sight would voluntarily choose to abandon visual interfaces entirely is not just unlikely. It contradicts our fundamental biology. Every new input modality we develop, voice, gesture, gaze, touch, maps back to a human sense. And every sense has a corresponding design layer that sits on top of it. That is not going to change because AI got better at understanding language. If anything the visual layer becomes more important as the underlying systems become more complex and harder to understand without one.

A Word About Accessibility

There is a version of this conversation that deserves its own space and its own depth and I want to flag it here before I close. Not everyone interacts with the world the same way. According to the World Health Organization at least 2.2 billion people globally live with some form of vision impairment. By 2050 that number is projected to reach 1.8 billion people with significant impairment including 61 million cases of blindness.

For those people voice input, haptic feedback, screen readers, and alternative interaction models are not productivity tools. They are the difference between participating in the digital world and being excluded from it. And the emergence of AI that can genuinely understand natural language, context, and intent is the most significant accessibility development in the history of computing.

This is not UI going away. This is UI expanding to include people it has never served well. And it deserves a full post of its own which is exactly what is coming next.

The Pattern Has Never Broken

Command line to GUI. Feature phone to touchscreen. Text box to full AI platform. Every single time a powerful technology got a thoughtful interface built around it the market exploded. Every single time. Without exception. And every single time there were people in the technical community who saw the interface as unnecessary overhead, a concession to the uninitiated, a dumbing down of something pure.

Those people were always wrong. Not because interfaces are dumbed down. But because interfaces are what connect powerful technology to the full range of human beings who could benefit from it. That has always been the job. It remains the job now.

UI is not going anywhere. It is just getting better at knowing when to show up.
