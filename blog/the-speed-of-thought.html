I have been dictating to my AI tools for a while now. Not because someone told me to, not because I read an article about productivity hacks, but because it just felt faster and more natural. Turns out I was not alone, and the data is starting to catch up to what a lot of us have already figured out on our own.

If you read my last post about the morgue our industry has become, do not panic. The keyboard is not dying. But it is no longer the only answer.

What Is Everybody Whispering About

Walk into certain offices right now and you will notice something strange. People are murmuring at their screens. Gooseneck microphones are appearing on desks. Lavalier mics, the kind you would normally see clipped to a news anchor or a YouTuber, are showing up in open plan offices clipped to collar edges and lapels. This is not a niche behavior anymore. Bloomberg reported earlier this year that at some companies the whispering begins with a single employee and then spreads, with workers quietly ditching their keyboards to murmur instructions to their computers instead. What started as an early adopter curiosity is becoming an office wide pattern, and the hardware market is already responding.

Your Fingers Are Losing

Here is the thing about typing. We have always accepted it as the default way to interact with computers, but it has never been the most natural or the most efficient way humans communicate. Most people speak at 125 to 150 words per minute versus 40 to 60 words per minute typing, a two to three times speed gap that we have been leaving on the table for decades because the technology to bridge it was not good enough. Now it is. One developer averaged 125 words per minute using voice dictation, twice the average typing speed, and dictated nearly 300,000 words in five months across 77 different apps. That is not a productivity hack. That is a workflow transformation.

We have seen this before. When mobile keyboards made typing slow and clumsy, people did not just accept the friction. They invented a new language. Emojis became shorthand for entire emotional states, reactions, and quick responses that would have taken three sentences to write. It was faster. It was efficient. And it was a completely legitimate evolution of how people communicate. But you cannot write a business proposal in emojis. You cannot explain a complex idea with a thumbs up and a fire symbol. The new input method filled a real gap without replacing everything that came before it. Voice input is doing the same thing. It is extraordinarily fast for long form thinking, context rich prompting, and getting complex ideas out of your head and into a tool quickly. But it is not replacing the keyboard any more than emojis replaced the sentence. It is sitting alongside it, handling the things it is genuinely better at.

And it is not just speed. When you speak rather than type you naturally give AI tools longer prompts with more context and more detail. Better input produces better output. The bottleneck was never the AI. It was the keyboard.

From Discord to Lavalier Mics

Remember when Midjourney lived entirely in Discord and you had to type slash commands to generate an image? That felt cutting edge for about five minutes before people realized how tedious it was. The tools that survived and scaled were the ones that built real interfaces around the core capability. Voice input is following the exact same arc. What started as a novelty is becoming infrastructure. Google just quietly released an offline first dictation app called AI Edge Eloquent on iOS, joining a rapidly growing category that already includes Wispr Flow, SuperWhisper, Willow, and Typeless. These are not experimental side projects. These are products people are building entire workflows around.

The lavalier microphone detail is my favorite part of all of this because it tells you everything about where human behavior goes when the technology finally gets good enough. People did not wait for a purpose built product. They borrowed hardware from a completely different industry, clipped it to their collar, and started talking to their computers. That is not a trend. That is a signal.

This Is Still a UX Problem

Here is where it gets interesting from a design perspective. Voice input does not eliminate the need for thoughtful UX. It creates an entirely new set of design challenges that we are only just beginning to map.

How do you design for input that is messy, non-linear, and full of false starts? How do you handle errors in a voice driven workflow without making the correction experience worse than just typing would have been? How do you give users confidence that what they said was what the system heard? How do you design history, memory, and organization around spoken interactions rather than typed ones? These are not small questions. They are the next frontier of interaction design and they require the same fundamental skills that good UX has always required. Empathy. Clarity. An understanding of how people actually behave versus how we assume they will.

The Bloomberg piece noted that voice mode is gaining traction with early adopters drawn to the promise of ever greater productivity. Early adopters are always the canary. What they figure out today becomes the mainstream expectation tomorrow. And when that happens the products that have thought deeply about the voice experience will separate from the ones that just bolted a microphone button onto an existing chat interface and called it a day.

The Input Method Changes. The Need for Design Does Not.

This is the throughline across everything happening in AI right now. The interface evolves. The underlying need for thoughtful, human centered design does not. We went from command lines to graphical interfaces. We went from desktop to mobile. We went from typed search queries to conversational AI. And now we are moving from typed prompts to spoken ones. Each transition created new patterns, new challenges, and new opportunities for design to matter.

Voice input is not the end of UI. It is not the end of the keyboard. It is a new input modality that sits alongside everything else and demands to be designed for with the same rigor and care we have always brought to the work. The tools are changing. The thinking behind them has not.

At the speed of thought, it turns out, is exactly where good UX has always been trying to get us.
